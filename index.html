Philosophy

Beliefs and mental models — concise, updated whenever I change my mind. Click any item to read more.

  • i.
    Creation is the highest form of meaning.
    Creation is broader than I once thought. It doesn't require paint, code, or instruments. Cooking a meal is creation. A well-crafted argument is creation. Structuring your day with intention is creation. Arranging a playlist for a specific mood is creation. What makes an act creative is not the medium but the intention — bringing something into being that didn't exist before, even if only in form or arrangement. Some examples that changed my definition:
    • A repair that restores something broken to wholeness
    • A conversation that opens up something previously unspoken
    • A notebook entry that finds, for the first time, what you actually think
    • A route through a city you've never walked
    This belief makes meaning accessible everywhere, not just in grand artistic projects. You are always, if you choose to be, in the act of creating something.
    +
  • ii.
    Everything is impermanent.
    This isn't pessimism — it's the most liberating fact I know. The difficult phase ends. So does the easy one. Clinging to either is the source of most suffering. I return to this when I'm too attached to an outcome, or too afraid to let something go. Impermanence is not loss. It's just time doing what time does.
    +
  • iii.
    You are not your thoughts.
    Thoughts arise automatically — unbidden, sometimes ugly, often irrational. You didn't choose them, so why identify with them? Noticing a thought without becoming it is the whole practice of meditation condensed into one sentence. This one took me a long time to actually feel rather than just intellectually understand. There's a gap between the thought and the self, and that gap is where freedom lives.
    +
  • iv.
    Create for its own sake, work for its own sake.
    The moment I began to learn guitar for the joy of sound rather than the imagined audience, I improved faster and cared less about how good I was. The moment I coded something because I genuinely needed it — not to put it on a portfolio — I built something I actually use. Extrinsic motivation is a short burn. Intrinsic is the long one. The outcome is a byproduct of the love, never the other way around.
    +
  • v.
    Don't let the figs rot and fall around you. Pick one, stay patient, and finish it — you'll find more that have ripened within reach.
    The fear of picking the "wrong" path — and thereby foreclosing all the other paths your imagination can generate — is paralyzing and universal. The fig tree metaphor (from Sylvia Plath's The Bell Jar) captures something real: an infinite field of ripening choices, and you, starving, unable to choose any of them. But the answer isn't to choose better. It's to choose at all, and trust that choosing teaches you more than deliberating ever will. Action reveals the next action. Inaction reveals nothing. And the figs you didn't pick? They weren't yours to begin with.
    +
  • vi.
    Systems that reward performance tend to destroy the love of the thing being performed.
    This is why I stopped treating grades as the unit of learning. Once I internalized the score, it colonized the thing. I started studying to perform on the test, not to understand the material. The same pattern shows up in athletics, art, and relationships. The extrinsic reward system is good at producing output. It is terrible at preserving love. Whenever I catch myself doing something primarily for validation, I treat that as a warning sign — not a motivation.
    +
  • vii.
    Boredom is information. It means you need harder problems, not easier distractions.
    When I feel bored, the instinct is to reach for stimulation — a phone, a video, anything. But boredom, investigated rather than escaped, almost always reveals something: I'm understimulated because the problem I'm working on isn't hard enough, or because I'm avoiding a harder one entirely. Boredom is the signal; distraction is what happens when you shoot the messenger instead of reading the message.
    +
  • viii.
    Almost every skill compounds. The question is whether you started.
    One year of anything done consistently with intention produces an outcome that surprises you. Two years produces one that surprises others. The reason most people don't experience this is they stop at month four. Compounding is a truth that everyone intellectually accepts and almost no one emotionally trusts. The emotional trust only comes after you've felt it once — which requires getting through the phase where it doesn't feel like it's working.
    +
  • ix.
    Dopamine is not a reward — it is a prediction. Hack the prediction, not the reward.
    Dopamine is released in anticipation of reward, not in response to it. This means the brain is not seeking satisfaction — it's seeking the prediction of satisfaction. If you associate deep work with anticipated reward (not instant gratification), the dopamine system works for you. If you train it on scrolling and novelty, it will always undermine your longer-term goals. The hack is not to remove pleasure — it's to redirect what the brain learns to anticipate.
    +
  • x.
    The notebook is not for recording what you know. It is for finding out what you think.
    Every time I sit down to write before I feel ready, I discover what I think. The notebook is not an archive — it's a lab. The act of putting words on a page forces a clarity that thinking-in-your-head never demands. Half-formed thoughts remain half-formed until they meet resistance: the resistance of language, of sequence, of committing to one word over another. This is why I write things down even when I have no intention of publishing or keeping them.
    +
  • xi.
    To be a beginner again, in everything — this is the highest aspiration I can name.
    Expertise brings efficiency but also expectation and ego. The beginner gets to be wrong without shame. The beginner gets to ask the basic questions — the ones that, once you're advanced, feel embarrassing to ask even though they're often the most important ones. The beginner sees things the expert no longer notices because they're too busy being correct. Every new thing I start — Farsi, Muay Thai, guitar — reminds me what it feels like to not know. I want to keep knowing that feeling.
    +

Stream of Consciousness Journals

Raw. Unedited. Click to read.

There's something deeply unsettling about the fact that a matrix has preferred directions. Like the universe has a bias built in. I keep trying to explain this to myself in plain English and it keeps dissolving back into symbols. Maybe that's the point. Maybe the symbol is the understanding, and the plain English is just the comfort blanket.
I was looking for it in my Farsi dictionary. They have دلتنگی — daltangi — which is closer to "my heart is tight" or "I miss you" but it's not quite nostalgia. It's more immediate. Maybe every language's untranslatable word is a window into what that culture aches for. Farsi aches for the present. English aches for the past.
I forgot what it sounds like. My own footsteps. The specific rhythm of breath when you're at that edge between comfortable and not. I've been filling that silence for years. Turns out it's mostly nothing. Which is the point, I think. The nothing is the thing.
Every time I pick up a new framework I feel capable and then I feel hollow. Like I built a sandcastle with a mold. I know it technically looks like a castle. But I didn't learn anything about sand. I didn't learn anything about castles. I keep going back to HTML and vanilla JS not out of nostalgia but because it forces me to know what I'm doing.

    Questions

    I believe communication is a medium of art — a bridge between unique universes that transforms a simple "hello" into a profound bond. Whether I'm joining someone eating solo or chatting with a neighbor in class, I find beauty in uncovering the hidden stories and perspectives every stranger carries. Inspired by the raw, wandering dialogues of the Before Trilogy, I've curated these questions to skip the small talk and reach the true essence of who someone is.

    Self & Identity
    • 1.When do you feel most like yourself — and when do you feel furthest from it?
    • 2.What's something you believe that most people around you don't?
    • 3.Is there a version of yourself you've left behind that you sometimes miss?
    • 4.What have you changed your mind about in the last few years — and what changed it?
    • 5.What part of yourself do you hide most often, and why?
    • 6.What would you do differently if you genuinely stopped caring what others thought?
    Depth & Desire
    • 7.What do you want that you've never told anyone?
    • 8.What's the most alive you've ever felt?
    • 9.What are you currently trying to figure out — in life, not just work?
    • 10.What do you think about just before you fall asleep?
    • 11.Is there something you've been meaning to say to someone but haven't?
    • 12.What's a question you're afraid of being asked?
    Connection & Others
    • 13.What does home feel like to you — is it a place, a person, or a state of mind?
    • 14.Who has shaped you most, and do they know it?
    • 15.What do you wish people asked you more about?
    • 16.What's the kindest thing a stranger has ever done for you?
    • 17.What do you need from people that you rarely ask for?
    Wonder & Ordinary Life
    • 18.What's a small thing that reliably makes you feel like the world is okay?
    • 19.If you had unlimited time, what subject would you study for the rest of your life?
    • 20.What would a perfect ordinary day look like for you?
    • 21.What's the most interesting place you've ever been — and what made it interesting?
    • 22.What's something you do for no reason other than that it makes you happy?
    • 23.What's something you've never been able to explain to someone else but you understand completely?
